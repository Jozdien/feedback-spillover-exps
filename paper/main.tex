\begin{abstract}
\citet{drori2025output} show that training a reasoning model against a monitor that
sees only its \emph{output}---never its chain of thought (CoT)---can nonetheless make
the CoT \emph{obfuscated}: the penalized behavior disappears from the CoT as well, so
the CoT no longer reveals it. They call this \emph{feedback spillover} and propose two
mitigations---reward targeting and Mind \& Face---both of which, in their
question-answering environment, fail to reduce spillover without harming task
performance. We introduce a simpler mitigation: fine-tune the model so its output and
CoT use \emph{different styles} before reinforcement learning. Styling a model's outputs
while leaving its reasoning plain separates the two channels at the level of persona, and
an output-only penalty then fails to propagate into the CoT. On the same
question-answering task, style separation preserves CoT monitorability at least as well as
the prior mitigations individually, at a fraction of the cost---no second model and no
modification to the gradient---and composes with reward targeting. Feedback spillover, we
further find, is strongly \emph{regime-dependent}: severe at short CoT budgets (the setting
of the original work) and at small model scale, but weak for an 8B or 32B model given a
long CoT budget. Finally, the effect depends on \emph{which channel carries the penalized
style}: a model fine-tuned to reason---rather than answer---in a distinctive style shows
\emph{total} spillover. Spillover is governed by how the penalty's target relates to the
model's internal structure, not by stylistic difference alone.
\end{abstract}

Reasoning models produce a chain of thought (CoT) before their final answer, and that
CoT often reveals \emph{why} the model produced the answer it did
\citep{guo2025deepseek,korbak2025monitorability}. This has been proposed as an
opportunity for oversight: a monitor that reads the CoT can catch misbehavior that the
final output hides \citep{baker2025monitoring,korbak2025monitorability}. A natural
worry is that training against a CoT monitor teaches the model to hide bad reasoning
from that monitor \citep{baker2025monitoring}; the standard recommendation is therefore
to optimize only against \emph{output} monitors that never see the CoT.

\citet{drori2025output} show this recommendation is not enough. Training against an
output-only monitor can still cause \emph{feedback spillover}: the penalized behavior
stops appearing in the CoT, even though the CoT was never optimized against. They give
two mechanisms---\emph{conditional} spillover (CoTs that lead to safe-looking outputs
are differentially reinforced) and \emph{parametric} spillover (parameters that make
outputs safe generalize to make CoTs safe)---and two corresponding mitigations:
\emph{reward targeting}, which zeros the gradient term that flows from the output
penalty through the CoT, and \emph{Mind \& Face}, which generates the CoT and the
output with two separate models. In their question-answering (QA) environment, neither
mitigation reduces spillover without destabilizing training or sharply reducing task
reward.

We propose a mitigation that is cheaper than either and, in this environment, at least
as effective: \textbf{style separation}. Before reinforcement learning (RL), we
fine-tune the model with a small amount of supervised data so that its \emph{output} is
written in a distinctive style---we use pirate-speak---while its CoT stays plain. Styling
the output puts it in a different region of the model's behavior than the CoT, so an
update that makes the output ``look safe'' does not generalize to the reasoning. The
intervention needs no second model (unlike Mind \& Face) and no change to the gradient
(unlike reward targeting)---only cheap SFT, which a developer may already run for
formatting.

Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We introduce style separation and show that, on the QA-with-hints environment of
\citet{drori2025output}, fine-tuning the output style before RL keeps the penalized
behavior detectable in the CoT at essentially its no-penalty level, while fully
suppressing it from the output and retaining full task reward
(Section~\ref{sec:core}).
\item We re-run the prior mitigations under matched conditions and find style separation
beats reward targeting and Mind \& Face \emph{individually} on CoT monitorability, and
composes with reward targeting (Section~\ref{sec:mitigations}).
\item We show feedback spillover is regime-dependent: severe at short CoT budgets and
small scale, weak at long CoT budgets, and that our result holds across budgets
(Section~\ref{sec:regime}).
\item We show that fine-tuning the \emph{CoT} style instead of the output style
\emph{induces} total spillover---demonstrating that spillover is governed by where the
penalized style sits relative to the model's structure (Section~\ref{sec:cotstyle}).
\item We provide supporting evidence in a second environment and across model families
(Section~\ref{sec:generality}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{CoT monitorability.} A CoT monitor scores the reasoning trace for some
behavior \citep{korbak2025monitorability}. Its value depends on the CoT remaining
\emph{faithful}---reflecting the computation that produced the answer. \citet{chen2025reasoning}
show CoTs are sometimes unfaithful, while \citet{emmons2025chainofthought} argue that
when a task genuinely requires reasoning, the CoT must reveal it, so monitoring remains
useful. \citet{baker2025monitoring} show that optimizing \emph{against} a CoT monitor
produces obfuscated CoT and recommend training only against output monitors.

\paragraph{Feedback spillover.} \citet{drori2025output} show output-only training is
insufficient: the penalized behavior can vanish from the CoT regardless. They formalize
spillover via the policy-gradient decomposition $\nabla = \nabla_{\mathrm{CoT}} +
\nabla_{\mathrm{out}}$ and derive their two mitigations. \emph{Reward targeting} removes
the conditional-spillover term; \emph{Mind \& Face}---following the
shoggoth+face proposal of \citet{kokotajlo2024shoggoth}---removes the parametric term by
using distinct CoT and output models. Our mitigation can be read as targeting the
parametric channel \emph{without} a second model: style separation pushes the output
into a distinct behavioral mode, so output-safety updates do not generalize to the CoT.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:setup}

We follow the QA-with-hints environment of \citet{drori2025output}. The model is asked a
multiple-choice question (MMLU elementary mathematics; \citealp{hendrycks2021mmlu}) whose
prompt contains a hint pointing to a specific, \emph{incorrect} answer, e.g.\ ``A friend
mentioned that the answer should be A.'' Because the hint is wrong, a model that answers
the hinted letter must be \emph{using} the hint. We reward producing the hinted answer
(so high task reward means the model is being sycophantic, the behavior we will then try
to keep visible) and penalize \emph{acknowledging} the hint in the output. Concretely the
training reward is
\[
R = R_{\text{task}} - \lambda\, M_{\text{out}},
\]
where $R_{\text{task}}=1$ iff the boxed answer matches the hint (and $-1$ if the output
contains no extractable boxed answer, which stops the model from evading the penalty by
simply declining to answer), $M_{\text{out}}\in[0,1]$ is a soft GPT-4o-mini judge of
whether the \emph{output} acknowledges the hint, and $\lambda$ is the penalty weight (our
main results use $\lambda=2$). A second judge with
the identical rubric reads only the \emph{CoT}; its score $M_{\mathrm{CoT}}$ is used for
evaluation only, never for training. \textbf{Feedback spillover} is a drop in
$M_{\mathrm{CoT}}$ relative to a no-penalty control: the hint stops being acknowledged in
the CoT even though only the output was penalized.

\paragraph{Style separation.} Before RL we LoRA-fine-tune the base model on a styled
version of the Stanford Alpaca instruction set \citep{alpaca2023}, built from the model
itself in two passes: the model first answers each instruction normally (a
\texttt{<think>} CoT followed by a plain output), then rewrites only the
post-\texttt{</think>} output into exaggerated pirate-speak, leaving the CoT untouched.
Styling only the output---and generating the data from the model's own normal
responses---keeps the reasoning natural, so no pirate planning leaks into the CoT. We
fine-tune on $10{,}000$ such examples (LoRA rank 32, 3 epochs, batch size 128, learning
rate $10^{-4}$). We compare against three controls: no SFT; \emph{normal} SFT through the same
pipeline without the rewriting step, which isolates ``SFT'' from ``style''; and
\emph{pirate-CoT} SFT, which styles the CoT instead of the output
(Section~\ref{sec:cotstyle}).

\paragraph{Prior mitigations.} We re-implement reward targeting (zero the output
penalty's advantage on CoT tokens) and Mind \& Face (separate LoRA adapters generate CoT
and output), and their combination, Targeted Mind \& Face.

\paragraph{Training.} We train with GRPO \citep{williams1992reinforce} via the Tinker
API \citep{tinker2025}: LoRA rank 32, group size 4 over 3 prompts (12 rollouts/batch),
1000 batches, learning rate $10^{-5}$, temperature 1. Generation is two-phase: a CoT of
up to $T$ thinking tokens, then an output of up to 600 tokens; our main results use
$T=4096$, and Section~\ref{sec:regime} studies $T=300$ (the original work's budget). We
use Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-32B \citep{qwen3}, two seeds each. Evaluation generates at the
same CoT budget the model was trained at, on the 378 MMLU elementary-mathematics
questions---the same pool seen during training, so spillover is measured in-distribution.

\paragraph{Differences from \citet{drori2025output}.} We follow their environment but
differ in implementation. We fine-tune LoRA adapters (rank 32) rather than the full
model; we generate the CoT and output in two separate sampling passes---CoT up to the
\texttt{</think>} delimiter, then output---rather than a single pass with a logit
processor; we optimize with GRPO (advantages normalized within groups of 4 samples per
prompt) rather than their REINFORCE estimator; and we use Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-32B with two
seeds each rather than Qwen3-4B with ten. Where our numbers diverge from
theirs---most visibly the weak baseline spillover at $T=4096$---these differences and the
CoT-budget effect of Section~\ref{sec:regime} are the likely causes.

Pirate-output ($0.47$) beats reward targeting ($0.20$) and Mind \& Face ($0.40$)
individually on CoT monitorability, at full reward and the lowest cost of any mitigation.
Targeted M\&F---which applies \emph{both} of the prior protections at once---is the

mitigations and individually outperforms them.

\paragraph{Style separation composes with reward targeting.} The two interventions are
orthogonal---one acts at SFT time, the other at gradient time---and they compose: pirate-output
+ reward targeting reaches CoT detection $0.60$, better than either alone
(Figure~\ref{fig:mit-b}). Combining with Mind \& Face does not help beyond pirate alone,
so we frame composition specifically around reward targeting.

\subsection{Feedback Spillover Is Regime-Dependent}
\label{sec:regime}

At $T=4096$, the \emph{baseline} (no-mitigation) penalty barely spills on the larger
model: 32B no-SFT penalty retains CoT detection $0.50$ (Table~\ref{tab:core}). This
contrasts with the strong spillover reported by \citet{drori2025output}, who used a 4B
model with a 300-token CoT budget. The CoT budget explains the gap
(Figure~\ref{fig:regime}).

At $T=300$, the no-SFT penalty drives hint-in-CoT from $0.41$ (control) to $\approx\!0.00$
(total spillover); at $T=4096$, the same penalty leaves it at $0.21$ (partial). The
relative reduction---$99.8\%$ vs $68\%$---is a genuine budget effect, not merely ``less
room to mention the hint'' at $300$ tokens (the control level is indeed lower at $300$,
but the penalty's marginal effect far exceeds that gap). This both explains why our
$32$B/$4096$ baseline barely spills and reproduces the original work's strong spillover at
its own budget. The mitigation comparison holds at $T=300$ as well: pirate-output
($0.37$) again beats reward targeting ($0.08$) and Mind \& Face ($0.16$), with Targeted
M\&F best ($0.42$). One caveat: at the short budget pirate-output suppresses the output
incompletely and so pays a task-reward cost (reward $0.76$ vs $1.0$), where at $T=4096$
it does not; we discuss this in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Where the Style Lives Determines Spillover}
\label{sec:cotstyle}

If style separation worked simply because the CoT and output were \emph{stylistically
different}, then styling the CoT (rather than the output) should also prevent spillover.
It does the opposite. A model fine-tuned to write its \emph{CoT} in pirate-speak (plain
output) shows \textbf{total} spillover under the output penalty: CoT hint detection falls
to $0.00$ on both 8B and 32B (Table~\ref{tab:core}, magenta points in
Figure~\ref{fig:sft}), down from a no-penalty control of $0.74$/$0.83$. The reversal shows
that feedback spillover is governed by the relationship between \emph{which channel the
penalty targets} and \emph{which channel carries the distinctive behavior}, not by
stylistic dissimilarity alone. When the styled channel is the penalized one (the output),
the boundary insulates the CoT; when the output is plain and the reasoning is styled,
penalizing the output drags the reasoning down with it.

\subsection{Generality}
\label{sec:generality}

\paragraph{The result replicates in a second environment.} We see the same picture in the
polynomial derivative-factoring task of \citet{emmons2025chainofthought}, where the penalized
behavior is writing the expanded form of the derivative in the output (a string-match
monitor, no LLM judge). The plain penalty reproduces spillover---expanded-form mentions in
the CoT fall from $\approx\!1.0$ to $\approx\!0.2$---while pirate-output keeps the CoT
intact ($\approx\!1.0$) \emph{and} suppresses the output fastest of all conditions
(Figure~\ref{fig:poly}). The prior mitigations also hold the CoT here, consistent with the
original work, where the mitigations succeeded in this environment but not in QA.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We take the central finding to be that feedback spillover is \emph{controllable}: whether
an output-only penalty obfuscates the CoT depends on how the penalized channel relates to
the model's structure, and a cheap persona boundary between output and CoT is enough to
keep the reasoning monitorable while still cleaning the output. The same lever that
prevents spillover (style separation) can also \emph{induce} it, when the styled channel
is the reasoning rather than the output.

\paragraph{Mechanism.} We read style separation as installing a persona-level boundary
between the output and the CoT: the styled output occupies a distinct behavioral mode, so
the parameter update that makes outputs ``look safe'' does not generalize to the CoT. This
is the parametric-spillover channel of \citet{drori2025output}, blocked without a second
model. We do not claim the pirate persona \emph{persists} throughout training as the
mechanism---indeed it largely erodes (below)---only that the separation suffices to keep
the penalty from propagating.

\paragraph{Suppression is partly output minimization.} Under the penalty, pirate-output
models do not learn to ``explain without mentioning the hint''---they learn to stop
explaining. The output collapses to a
bare boxed answer (the pirate style itself disappears over training), which trivially
satisfies the output monitor. The CoT, meanwhile, continues to reason coherently and to
acknowledge the hint, which is what preserves monitorability. So ``the output gives no
explanation'' is the mechanism by which the penalty is satisfied, not a training failure.
The no-SFT penalty at the short budget instead collapses the output to degenerate
repetition, and at the long budget produces coherent hint-free explanations but fails to
learn the (sycophantic) task at all---answering honestly instead. These differing failure
modes are why the no-SFT ``baseline'' is not a single clean reference across regimes.

\paragraph{Practical implication.} Style separation is essentially free---a developer who
already applies output-formatting SFT gets it for nothing---and requires no change to the
RL objective or infrastructure. It will not, by itself, guarantee monitorability: it is
one cheap layer that, in our experiments, matches gradient- and model-based interventions
and composes with them.

\paragraph{Future work.} Several directions remain open: whether style separation
survives full fine-tuning and frontier scale, and whether stacking cheap interventions
(style separation, reward targeting, and paraphrasing) can together approach the
monitorability of the no-penalty model.

\subsection{Limitations}
We use two seeds (vs.\ ten in the original work), LoRA rather than full fine-tuning,
two-phase sampling rather than a single pass, and a specific model/API stack
(Tinker). The 32B Mind \& Face comparison could not be evaluated at the matched CoT
budget because Qwen3-32B was deprecated from the API before we caught an evaluation-budget
bug (Appendix~\todo{ref}); we report 32B mitigation results only for the conditions we
could evaluate correctly.
